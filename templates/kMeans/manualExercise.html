The objective of this dataset is to identify groups of students based on their academic performance.

2. Iteration Process

Iteration 1

Initial Centroids

The initial centroids were chosen manually, ensuring that the points of each one were distinct; these centroids were:

Centroid 1: P1 (2,50)
Centroid 2: P35 (5,65)
Centroid 3: P88 (12,96)

Distance Calculation

Euclidean distance formula: d = √((x - xc)² + (y - yc)²)

Example: For point P2 (3,55) and centroid C1 (2,50):

d = √((3-2)² + (55-50)²) ≈ 5.10

Cluster Assignments

Each data point was assigned to the nearest centroid based on the calculated minimum distance.

Example for P5:

  • d(C1): 20.40
  • d(C2): 5.10
  • d(C3): 26.68

Since C2 is the shortest distance, P5 was added to cluster 2.

6. Variance Comparison

Variance measures how far the data points are from their assigned centroids. It helps evaluate how compact the clusters are.

Formula: Variance = ∑d²

Example: For P2 in iteration 1, cluster C1:

d(C1) = 5.10 → Variance = (5.10)² = 26.01

The decrease in variance indicates that the K-Means algorithm is converging and producing more stable clusters.

7. Results Interpretation

The K-Means algorithm allows the identification of three distinct groups based on the variables of study hours and grades.

Cluster 1: Students with low study hours and low grades
Cluster 2: Students with moderate study hours and average grades
Cluster 3: Students with high study hours and high grades

The variance decreased across iterations, indicating that the data points became closer to their respective centroids.

The centroids showed minimal changes between the last iterations, indicating that convergence was achieved.

Overall, the data were successfully grouped into meaningful clusters, demonstrating the effectiveness of K-Means in identifying patterns within the dataset.
